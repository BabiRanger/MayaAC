The "j" Operator (Phase Rotation)

In pure mathematics, the imaginary unit is called $i$ ($\sqrt{-1}$). However, in electrical engineering, we use $j$ to avoid confusion with alternating current ($i$).

  • The Math: $j = \sqrt{-1}$ and $j^2 = -1$.
  • The Physics: Multiplying any vector by $j$ acts as a rotation operator. It rotates the vector exactly $90^\circ$ counter-clockwise in the complex plane.
  • The Complex Plane: The horizontal axis is the Real part (Resistance), and the vertical axis is the Imaginary part (Reactance).

Fig 3. Multiplying by j causes a $90^\circ$ phase shift

The $+j$ Rotation

Rotates the vector $90^\circ$ Counter-Clockwise.

$$ +j = 1 \angle 90^\circ $$
Used for Inductors ($+jX_L$)

The $-j$ Rotation

Rotates the vector $90^\circ$ Clockwise.

$$ -j = 1 \angle -90^\circ $$
Used for Capacitors ($-jX_C$)

Putting it together (Rectangular Form)

When you see a total impedance calculated as $\mathbf{Z} = 3 + j4 \ \Omega$, it simply means the circuit has $3\Omega$ of standard resistance pushing along the horizontal axis, and $4\Omega$ of net inductive reactance pushing $90^\circ$ UP on the vertical axis!
