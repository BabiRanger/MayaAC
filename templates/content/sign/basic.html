Understanding Passive Sign Convention

Explaining Power Sign based on Terminal and Current Direction

The Passive Sign Convention (PSC) is a standard rule used in electrical engineering to determine the sign of power ($p$) calculated for a circuit element. It ensures we consistently interpret whether an element is absorbing or supplying energy.

The Two Golden Rules

The sign of the power depends entirely on the direction the current enters the voltage terminal:

1. Absorbing Power

If current flows into the Positive (+) terminal:

$$ p = +vi $$

Implication: If $p > 0$, the element is absorbing power (consuming energy), like a Resistor.

2. Supplying Power

If current flows into the Negative (-) terminal:

$$ p = -vi $$

Implication: If $p < 0$, the element is supplying power (delivering energy), like a Battery.

Why use this convention?

Without PSC, we wouldn't know if a calculated power value meant the component was a source (battery) or a load (lightbulb). PSC keeps the meaning consistent:

  • Positive Power ($+$): Energy is being taken out of the circuit (e.g., charging a battery, heating a resistor).
  • Negative Power ($-$): Energy is being put into the circuit (e.g., a discharging battery).

Note: This same formula works for both passive and active elements!
